The Water Cycle

The water cycle describes how water moves continuously through the Earth's
environment. It has no beginning and no end; water simply changes form and
location as energy from the Sun drives it around a loop.

Evaporation is the first stage: heat from the Sun turns liquid water in oceans,
lakes, and rivers into water vapour, which rises into the atmosphere. Plants add
to this through transpiration, releasing water vapour from their leaves.

As the vapour rises it cools and condenses into tiny droplets, forming clouds.
When those droplets combine and grow heavy enough, they fall back to the surface
as precipitation: rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

Some of the water that reaches the ground flows over the surface as runoff,
collecting into streams and rivers that carry it back to the sea. The rest soaks
into the soil as infiltration, replenishing the groundwater stored in underground
aquifers.

Because the total amount of water on Earth stays essentially constant, the water
you drink today has cycled through this system countless times before.
